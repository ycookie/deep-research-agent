# Research Report: Impact of Remote Work on Urban Commercial Real Estate (2022-2025)

**Date:** 2025-11-15 | **Sources consulted:** 8

## Executive Summary

Remote and hybrid work arrangements have permanently reduced demand for traditional office space in major US cities, with average office vacancy rates reaching 20% nationally by mid-2025. However, the impact varies dramatically by city, building class, and neighborhood, with some markets showing early signs of recovery through adaptive reuse and flight-to-quality trends.

## Key Findings

### Office Vacancy and Utilization

- National office vacancy rates reached approximately 20% by mid-2025, up from roughly 12% pre-pandemic (Confidence: HIGH)
- Average office utilization on peak days hovers around 60% of pre-2020 levels, with Tuesdays through Thursdays seeing the most traffic (Confidence: HIGH)
- Class B and C office buildings are disproportionately affected, with vacancy rates significantly higher than Class A properties in the same markets (Confidence: HIGH)

### Geographic Variation

- San Francisco and other tech-heavy metros have experienced the steepest declines in office occupancy and commercial property values (Confidence: HIGH)
- Sun Belt cities including Austin, Miami, and Nashville have seen comparatively milder impacts, partly offset by population growth (Confidence: MEDIUM)
- Suburban office parks have generally outperformed downtown core properties, reversing the pre-pandemic trend (Confidence: MEDIUM)

### Adaptive Reuse and Conversion

- Office-to-residential conversion has emerged as a major trend, though only a fraction of vacant office space is structurally suitable for conversion (Confidence: HIGH)
- Federal and local governments have introduced incentive programs to encourage conversions, with varying success (Confidence: MEDIUM)
- The conversion process faces significant challenges including zoning, building codes, floor plate dimensions, and financing (Confidence: HIGH)

### Financial Impact

- Commercial mortgage-backed securities (CMBS) tied to office properties have seen elevated delinquency and special servicing rates (Confidence: HIGH)
- Some regional banks with concentrated commercial real estate exposure have faced financial stress (Confidence: MEDIUM)
- Property tax revenue declines have affected municipal budgets in office-dependent districts (Confidence: MEDIUM)

### Emerging Trends

- "Flight to quality" — tenants are consolidating into smaller but higher-quality spaces with better amenities (Confidence: HIGH)
- Flexible/coworking space continues to grow as companies adopt hybrid models (Confidence: MEDIUM)
- Some landlords are repositioning buildings as mixed-use, adding retail, fitness, and hospitality elements (Confidence: LOW — early trend with limited data)

## Areas of Debate or Uncertainty

**Will office demand recover?** Sources disagree on the long-term trajectory. Some commercial real estate firms project a gradual recovery as companies mandate return-to-office. Labor economists and workplace researchers counter that hybrid work is a permanent structural shift, and pre-pandemic occupancy levels will not return.

**Scale of the financial risk.** Some analysts warn that commercial real estate stress could trigger broader financial instability, particularly for regional banks. Others argue the risk is manageable and overstated by media, noting that most large banks have limited direct exposure.

**Effectiveness of conversion incentives.** Early data on office-to-residential conversion programs is mixed, and it's too soon to tell whether government incentives will meaningfully accelerate the pace of conversion.

## Knowledge Gaps

- **International comparison**: This research focused on US markets. How remote work has affected commercial real estate in Europe and Asia may differ significantly.
- **Small and mid-size cities**: Most available data covers major metros. The impact on secondary and tertiary markets is less documented.
- **Long-term tenant behavior**: It's unclear how office lease decisions will evolve as current leases signed pre-pandemic continue to expire through 2026-2028.
- **Environmental implications**: The carbon footprint implications of partially empty office buildings vs. distributed home offices were not well covered in the sources found.
