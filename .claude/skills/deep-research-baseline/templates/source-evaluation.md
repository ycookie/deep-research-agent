# Source Evaluation Criteria

Use this checklist when assessing source quality during Phase 3 of the research workflow.

## 1. Authority

- [ ] **Known publication** — Is this from an established, recognized outlet (major newspaper, academic journal, industry publication)?
- [ ] **Expert author** — Does the author have credentials or demonstrated expertise in this subject?
- [ ] **Institutional backing** — Is the source affiliated with a university, research institute, government agency, or recognized organization?
- [ ] **Editorial oversight** — Does the publication have editors, peer review, or fact-checking processes?

**Score: Strong / Moderate / Weak / Unknown**

## 2. Recency

- [ ] **Publication date identified** — Can you determine when this was published or last updated?
- [ ] **Appropriate timeliness** — Is the content recent enough given how fast this topic evolves?
- [ ] **Data currency** — Are the statistics and data points from recent enough time periods?

Context matters: A 2020 article about the Roman Empire is fine; a 2020 article about AI capabilities is outdated.

**Score: Current / Acceptable / Outdated / Unknown**

## 3. Evidence Type

Rate the strength of evidence presented:

| Type | Strength |
|------|----------|
| Original peer-reviewed research | Strongest |
| Government or institutional data | Strong |
| Primary source documents | Strong |
| Expert analysis with citations | Moderate-Strong |
| Investigative journalism | Moderate-Strong |
| Industry reports | Moderate |
| News reporting | Moderate |
| Opinion/editorial with evidence | Moderate-Weak |
| Blog post or opinion piece | Weak |
| Social media or forum | Weakest |

**Evidence type: [identify]**

## 4. Bias Indicators

Check for potential bias:

- [ ] **Advocacy organization** — Is the publisher an advocacy group with a stated position on this topic?
- [ ] **Commercial interest** — Is the source selling something related to the topic? (vendor whitepapers, product-affiliated blogs)
- [ ] **Political lean** — Does the outlet have a known political orientation that could color coverage?
- [ ] **Funding source** — Is the research funded by parties with a stake in the outcome?
- [ ] **Conflict of interest** — Does the author have financial or professional ties to one side?

Bias doesn't disqualify a source, but it should be noted and factored into confidence assessments.

**Bias risk: Low / Moderate / High**

## 5. Independence

- [ ] **Original reporting** — Does this source present original research, data, or analysis?
- [ ] **Attribution** — Does it cite its own sources, or make unsupported claims?
- [ ] **Unique perspective** — Does it add something beyond what other sources say?
- [ ] **Not just aggregation** — Is this more than a rewrite of other articles or press releases?

**Independence: Original / Partially original / Aggregated**

---

## Quick Assessment Summary

After checking the above, assign an overall rating:

- **Tier 1 (High credibility):** Strong authority + current + strong evidence + low bias + original
- **Tier 2 (Good credibility):** Moderate authority + acceptable recency + decent evidence + some bias risk
- **Tier 3 (Use with caution):** Weak authority OR outdated OR opinion-based OR high bias
- **Tier 4 (Avoid or flag):** Unknown authority + no evidence + high bias + pure aggregation

Use Tier 1-2 sources as primary evidence. Tier 3 sources can provide perspective but should not be sole support for claims. Tier 4 sources should generally be skipped unless they contain unique primary data.
